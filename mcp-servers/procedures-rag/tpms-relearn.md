# TPMS Sensor Relearn Procedures

## When a TPMS relearn is needed

After tire rotation (on vehicles that track sensor position), sensor replacement, or installing a winter wheel set with new sensors. Symptom of a missing relearn: TPMS light on or flashing, or the dash showing pressures in the wrong corners.

## GM trucks and SUVs (2014+)

1. Ignition on, engine off. Press and hold the lock and unlock buttons on the key fob simultaneously until the horn chirps twice — the left front turn signal illuminates, indicating learn mode. (Alternative entry: through the DIC Tire Pressure menu.)
2. Starting at the LEFT FRONT, use a TPMS activation tool at the valve stem. The horn chirps once when the sensor registers.
3. Proceed in order: left front, right front, right rear, left rear.
4. After the fourth sensor, the horn chirps twice and learn mode exits. Set all four tires to placard pressure.

## Ford F-150 and Super Duty

1. Ignition off. Press and release the brake pedal.
2. Cycle the ignition from off to run three times, ending in run. Press and release the brake pedal again. Cycle off-to-run three more times, ending in run.
3. The horn chirps once and the message center shows "Train left front tire."
4. Activate each sensor with the tool in the order prompted: left front, right front, right rear, left rear. Horn chirps after each.
5. A double chirp after the last wheel confirms completion.

## Toyota / Lexus

Most Toyotas do not track position — they only need a pressure-warning reset after adjusting pressures: with ignition on, hold the TPMS reset button (under the dash, left of the steering column) until the TPMS light blinks three times, then drive for 10-20 minutes. After replacing sensors, the new sensor IDs must be registered with a scan tool (Techstream or equivalent); there is no fob or pedal dance.

## RAM trucks

RAM trucks auto-learn: drive above 15 mph for 10+ minutes and the system picks up new sensor IDs on its own. No tool required for a simple rotation — positions update automatically.

## Notes on sensor batteries

TPMS sensor batteries last 5-10 years and are not replaceable. When one sensor in a set dies, quote the customer for replacing all four — the rest are the same age, and chasing them one at a time costs the customer more in mount/balance labor than batching.
