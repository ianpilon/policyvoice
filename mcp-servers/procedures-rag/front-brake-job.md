# Front Brake Pad and Rotor Replacement

## Overview and safety

Standard front disc brake service: pads and rotors on a hub-mounted rotor design (covers most light trucks and passenger cars). Always service brakes in axle pairs. Never let the caliper hang by its hose. Brake dust: wet-clean, never compressed air.

## Disassembly

### Wheel and caliper removal

1. Break lug nuts loose on the ground, then lift and support the vehicle on jack stands at factory lift points.
2. Remove the wheel. Inspect the rotor for scoring, heat checking, and measure thickness against the discard spec stamped on the rotor hat.
3. Remove the two caliper guide pin bolts. Pivot or lift the caliper off and hang it from the spring or a hook — never by the hose.
4. Remove the pads from the bracket. Note the position of wear-indicator pads (usually inboard).
5. Remove the two caliper bracket bolts (these are the high-torque bolts — see the spec database for your vehicle) and remove the bracket.
6. Pull the rotor. If rusted to the hub, use the threaded jack-screw holes in the rotor hat or strike the hat (not the friction surface) with a dead-blow.

## Preparation and reassembly

### Hub and hardware prep

1. Wire-brush or flap-disc the hub face to bare metal. Rust scale between hub and rotor causes lateral runout, which becomes pedal pulsation within 5,000 miles. This step matters more than rotor brand.
2. Clean the bracket abutment clips or replace with the hardware kit. Apply a thin film of synthetic brake grease where the pad ears ride.
3. Pull the guide pins, wipe, inspect the boots, and re-lube with silicone brake grease. A seized pin is the most common cause of one-sided pad wear.

### Pad and rotor install

1. New rotor: clean the anti-corrosion coating off the friction surfaces with brake cleaner.
2. Install the bracket and torque the bracket bolts to spec. Apply threadlocker only if the service manual calls for it.
3. Compress the caliper piston with a piston tool against the old pad. On electronic parking brake rears, the piston must be retracted with a scan tool in service mode — do not force it.
4. Install pads, set the caliper over them, and torque the guide pin bolts to spec.
5. Wheel on, torque lugs to spec in a star pattern. Never finish lug nuts with an impact.

## After the job

### Bedding and verification

1. Pump the pedal to a firm height BEFORE moving the vehicle.
2. Bed the pads: six to ten moderate stops from 35 mph with cooling distance between, no full ABS stops, then avoid hard braking for 100 miles.
3. Check brake fluid level — it rises when pistons are compressed; remove excess rather than letting it overflow.
4. Re-torque lug nuts after 50 miles on aluminum wheels.
