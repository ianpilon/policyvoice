# Serpentine Belt Inspection and Replacement

## Inspection standards

### EPDM belts don't crack — they wear

Modern EPDM belts rarely show the classic cracking of old neoprene belts. They wear like a tire: the rib valleys widen and lose material. Use a belt wear gauge in the rib grooves — if the gauge tool sits flush or below the rib peaks, the belt is worn out regardless of how clean it looks. Typical EPDM service life is 90,000-100,000 miles.

### Noise diagnosis before replacement

A chirp (rhythmic, RPM-dependent) is misalignment — check pulley alignment with a laser tool or straightedge before throwing a belt at it. A squeal (load-dependent, worse with A/C on or wet) is slip — check tensioner force and belt wear. Replacing a belt for a chirp without fixing alignment brings the truck back in a week.

## Replacement procedure

1. Photograph or sketch the belt routing before removal, or confirm against the routing decal under the hood. On twin-belt engines, note which belt drives which accessories.
2. Rotate the automatic tensioner off the belt with a serpentine belt tool or long-handle ratchet. Slip the belt off the nearest smooth idler first, then release the tensioner slowly — never let it snap back.
3. With the belt off, spin every pulley by hand. Listen for bearing growl on idlers and the tensioner pulley; check the A/C clutch bearing and alternator. A noisy idler found now saves a comeback.
4. Check the tensioner: it should sweep smoothly through its travel with firm, even spring force and no binding or wobble in the arm. Most tensioner wear is in the pivot bushing — wobble means replace the tensioner assembly.
5. Route the new belt per the diagram, leaving the smooth idler for last. Verify the belt is centered in every pulley's grooves — one rib off on a hidden pulley destroys the belt in minutes.
6. Run the engine and watch the belt and the tensioner arm. The arm should hold steady with only minor flutter; large oscillation means a failing tensioner or a misfiring engine driving torsional vibration.

## Stretch-fit belts

Some accessories (often A/C on transverse engines) use a stretch-fit belt with no tensioner. These are single-use: cut it off, and install the new one with the manufacturer's install tool that walks it over the pulley flange as you rotate the crank by hand. Never pry a stretch belt on with a screwdriver — it damages the cord and the belt fails early.
